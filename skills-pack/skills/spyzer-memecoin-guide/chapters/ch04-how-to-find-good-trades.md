# Chapter 4: How to Find Good Trades

## Core Idea
Good trades come from better information infrastructure, not passive following. A trader improves by building groups, feeds, research habits, and a thesis process that finds and filters coins before the crowd.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Solo fast, together far**: trusted group chats multiply discovery and verification.
  - When to use: when trying to improve trade sourcing.
  - How: create or join small groups where members share contract addresses, theses, red flags, and follow-up research.
- **Think first, follow second**: original thinking creates edge; copying arrives late.
  - When to use: whenever a call channel, KOL, or wallet alert suggests a trade.
  - How: ask what the trade is, why now, what others missed, and what invalidates it.
- **Information deficiency edge**: profit comes from knowing relevant bullish or bearish facts before most buyers.
  - When to use: after finding a fresh token or narrative.
  - How: search X, verify dev identity, check LinkedIn/Discord/social links, cross-check wallets, ask trusted groups, and look for facts not yet public.
- **Two-sentence thesis rule**: if you cannot state why you are buying and what changes your mind, you should not buy.
  - When to use: before every meaningful trade.
  - How: write the thesis and invalidation in a note or chat before entry.

## Key Concepts
- **Group chat edge**: distributed research among trusted traders.
- **Scanning bot**: Telegram bot that summarizes contract and holder data.
- **CA search**: searching a token contract address on X to find human discussion.
- **Human posts**: posts with actual reasoning, not automated call-channel bait.
- **Thesis**: a clear reason the market should reprice the coin.
- **Information asymmetry**: one side of the trade knows more than the other.

## Mental Models
- Use **infrastructure before impulse**: build the chat/feed/bot setup before you need it.
- Think of **X as public narrative formation** and Telegram as real-time coordination.
- Treat **low-follower early thinkers** as potentially more valuable than late large accounts.
- Use **would new information make buyers care?** as the standard for whether research matters.

## Anti-patterns
- **Waiting for someone bigger to tell you what to buy**: the follower is late by design.
- **Joining only public call channels**: they surface trades but rarely protect you from timing and exit mistakes.
- **Mistaking activity for edge**: more notifications do not equal better decisions.
- **Keeping a stale thesis**: if the reasons for holding changed, update size or exit.

## Worked Example
A fresh coin launches from an account claiming to build a neobank. Instead of aping the chart, research the dev's identity, mutual followers, LinkedIn history, related projects, whether the account is hacked, and comparable market caps. If credible information supports a valuation gap and the market has not priced it yet, the edge is real; if the facts are already public and price is high, the trade has changed.

## Key Takeaways
1. Build trusted information streams before deploying serious capital.
2. Search both contract address and ticker, and separate human posts from bots.
3. A clear thesis must precede the trade.
4. New information should constantly update whether you hold, reduce, or exit.
5. Good trading is repeated pattern recognition, not one lucky call.
