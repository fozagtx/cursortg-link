# Chapter 10: The Psychology of Trading

## Core Idea
Trading decisions fail when fear, scarcity, revenge, missed opportunities, or ego take control. The goal is not emotionless perfection; it is recognizing psychological failure modes early enough to reduce damage.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Fish & Monkey Methodology**: traders have different natural risk profiles.
  - When to use: when your style feels forced or your regrets repeat.
  - How: decide whether you naturally prefer steady compounding or high-variance moonshots; adapt tactically without betraying your core temperament.
- **Scarcity brain**: financial pressure makes every trade feel like the only chance.
  - When to use: when you hold too long, panic-sell, oversize, or refuse to redeploy.
  - How: build a financial floor, size like future opportunities are abundant, and stop trading from survival pressure.
- **Fumble spiral**: missing a winner damages the next trades.
  - When to use: after selling too early, not buying, or missing a huge runner.
  - How: pause, identify whether urgency is driving you, and return to process before entering again.
- **Awareness versus learning**: lessons become real only when applied during pressure.
  - When to use: after repeating a known mistake.
  - How: turn recognition into a rule and reduce the damage faster next time.
- **Accountability habit**: missed trades count as data, not just losses.
  - When to use: after any meaningful miss or fumble.
  - How: diagnose why it happened and change one behavior.

## Key Concepts
- **Fish**: trader type focused on steady gains and risk control.
- **Monkey**: trader type built for high-risk, high-reward conviction.
- **Revenge trading**: trying to recover losses quickly through worse trades.
- **Scarcity mindset**: belief that opportunities are rare and must be maximized or forced.
- **Fumble**: missing or mishandling a major opportunity.
- **Delusional optimism**: strong belief that continued effort will eventually find opportunity, balanced with risk control.

## Mental Models
- Use **build the floor first**: if survival pressure drives trading, step back and create income or savings outside crypto.
- Treat **missed runners as part of the job**, not proof that the next trade must be forced.
- Think **you are not making money back; you are making money**.
- Use **fun and flow**: stable, engaged traders notice more and last longer.

## Anti-patterns
- **Trading against your nature**: a fish pretending to be a monkey or vice versa.
- **Letting a missed trade dictate the next trade**: FOMO becomes strategy.
- **Calling awareness enough**: repeated mistakes need rule changes.
- **Ignoring life pressure**: trading while desperate makes clean decision-making nearly impossible.

## Worked Example
After a major loss, a trader feels pressure to recover quickly and starts buying weaker setups. The repair path is not a motivational speech: stop trading, identify the specific process failure, create a financial or emotional safety floor if needed, and only return to high-conviction trades with written sizing and invalidation rules.

## Key Takeaways
1. Know your trader temperament and stop copying incompatible styles.
2. Scarcity brain can distort both wins and losses.
3. Missing a trade is dangerous mainly because of what it does to your next decisions.
4. Every significant miss deserves honest review.
5. Optimism is useful when paired with process and risk control.
