# Chapter 9: Trade Execution

## Core Idea
Finding a good coin is only half the game. Most money is won or lost through thesis clarity, sizing, entries, profit-taking, loss cutting, and journaling.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Know why before buying**: a trade must have a written thesis and invalidation.
  - When to use: before every meaningful entry.
  - How: write why you are buying, why now, what would make you sell, and how new information changes the trade.
- **Current-size test**: compare what you hold now to what you would buy now.
  - When to use: after price or information changes.
  - How: if you would buy less at the current price, reduce the difference.
- **Sizing by conviction and survivability**: size enough to matter but not enough to damage the next decision.
  - When to use: before entering.
  - How: combine portfolio percentage, liquidity, confidence, information edge, and emotional tolerance.
- **Gradual profit-taking**: secure gains as the trade moves in your favor.
  - When to use: on significant unrealized profit.
  - How: sell portions into strength, derisk, and leave a runner only if the thesis still justifies it.
- **Loss-to-rule conversion**: every significant loss should produce a specific process rule.
  - When to use: after a bad trade.
  - How: identify oversizing, ignored red flags, poor exit plan, emotional entry, or missed information; write a constraint that prevents repeat damage.

## Key Concepts
- **Information deficiency edge**: relevant facts not yet priced by most traders.
- **Position sizing**: determining how much capital a trade deserves.
- **FOMO entry**: buying because price urgency overwhelms thesis.
- **Profit scaling**: selling portions over time instead of all at once.
- **Stop/invalidation**: condition that proves the trade idea wrong.
- **Journaling**: documenting trades and lessons to accelerate pattern recognition.

## Mental Models
- Use **if this goes to zero, can I trade normally tomorrow?** as a sizing gate.
- Think **confidence is not edge** unless backed by information.
- Use **would I buy this now?** to avoid holding stale launch theses.
- Treat **withdrawn profit as real profit**; wallet numbers can disappear.

## Anti-patterns
- **Oversizing on confidence**: one bad trade can create emotional and financial damage.
- **Spreading across too many coins**: diluted focus and diluted upside.
- **Chasing green candles without thesis**: urgency is not information.
- **Trying to make money back**: revenge trading anchors decisions to the past.
- **Vague post-loss lessons**: "be careful" does not change behavior.

## Worked Example
A coin bought on a fresh-launch information edge is up after several days. The original reasons have played out. Ask: would I buy this exact amount today at this price? If not, sell the difference. If you still hold, it must be because a new thesis exists, such as team execution, community growth, or product launch, not because the old entry story felt good.

## Key Takeaways
1. Trade management is more important than entry.
2. Size by portfolio, conviction, liquidity, and emotional tolerance.
3. Define entry and invalidation before the excitement starts.
4. Take profits while they are available.
5. Journal wins, losses, and missed trades that taught something.
