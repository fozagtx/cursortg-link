# Chapter 5: KOLs, Influencers & Trust

## Core Idea
Follower count is a weak proxy for trading skill. Influencers and call channels are useful only as information streams; the decision must come from your own research, track record assessment, and thesis.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Track record over followers**: judge a KOL by repeated behavior, not audience size.
  - When to use: before taking any public call seriously.
  - How: monitor calls over time, note deleted posts, updates, transparency, and whether losses are acknowledged.
- **Paid-deal awareness**: promotion can be compensated with money or token supply.
  - When to use: when several accounts suddenly post the same coin.
  - How: ask who was positioned first, whether the posts feel coordinated, and who benefits from your entry.
- **Do not borrow conviction**: public conviction is usually late and incomplete.
  - When to use: whenever someone else's confidence makes you want to buy.
  - How: reconstruct their possible entry, exit, size, and incentives; if you cannot, downgrade the signal.
- **Information stream, not buy signal**: KOLs can surface coins you missed.
  - When to use: when following X profiles, Telegram channels, or app leaders.
  - How: collect the lead, then run your own checklist before acting.

## Key Concepts
- **KOL**: key opinion leader or crypto influencer.
- **Call channel**: a feed where coins are posted as possible trades.
- **Exit liquidity**: buyers used by earlier holders to sell profitably.
- **Coordinated push**: multiple accounts posting to simulate organic attention.
- **Borrowed conviction**: trading from another person's thesis without the underlying reasoning.

## Mental Models
- Treat **large followings as liquidity**, not proof.
- Think of **public calls as delayed information**: by the time conviction is posted, positioning may already be complete.
- Use **behavioral audits**: deleted calls, no bearish updates, and constant wins-only posting are red flags.
- Prefer **transparent thesis writers** over accounts that only post green candles.

## Anti-patterns
- **Blind aping favorite channels**: even good channels can be wrong or late.
- **Trusting one viral win**: a lottery-ticket trade can manufacture credibility.
- **Ignoring incentives**: paid or supply-backed promotion changes the meaning of a post.
- **Following engagement metrics**: bot activity can simulate consensus.

## Worked Example
Two influencers post trades. One posts a coin at low market cap, explains why, updates with new information, and leaves losing calls visible. Another posts a coin, it spikes, crashes, and the post disappears. The first can become an information source; the second should be muted or blocked. Neither should replace your own thesis.

## Key Takeaways
1. Good KOLs are proven by time and transparency.
2. Followers and engagement are easy to misread.
3. Public conviction often means someone else is already positioned.
4. Use channels to discover, not decide.
5. If you cannot explain the trade independently, you do not own the conviction.
